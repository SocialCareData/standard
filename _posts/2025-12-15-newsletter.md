## Welcome

As 2025 wraps up, we’re sharing highlights from our time at NCASC 2025, and a powerful reminder of the importance of our work through our CYP and VCSE Engagement workshops. We’ll take a trip down memory lane to see what we’ve achieved this year and what’s in store for 2026.

A huge thank you to everyone we’ve worked with — your insight and energy continue to drive our important work forward.


## 1. NCASC 2025: Key Moments

### What we got up to

We joined colleagues across the sector at NCASC in Bournemouth, diving into sessions on our new data standards for describing children’s placement needs and addressing the challenges around multi-agency information sharing.

### Putting young people’s voices direct to decision-makers

It was inspiring to speak to so many DCS', system vendors and individuals committed to improving the lives of children and families. There, Harry was able to put a question about care leavers to Josh MacAlister, Minister for Children and Families. The Minister was full of praise for the work we're doing, and we were so pleased to so quickly share the experiences of the young people we engaged, with the person who has the most power to do something about it!

### Growing momentum for the Placements Standards

We also had a chance to showcase our placement standard. We had a lot of interest from LAs, VCSEs, researchers and others in the CSC space in what analysis it could enable. Even better, we’re now up to six regions interested in implementing the standard next year, a strong signal that we’re building something valuable (and maybe something others won’t want to miss!).

### Kinnock’s vision for care

The team was lucky to catch Stephen Kinnock (Minister of Health & Social Care) set out a clear ambition: a National Care Service with nationally high standards, locally delivered, so wherever you live, you can access the care you need to live the life you choose.

NCASC was a great opportunity for us to connect and learn, and we’re looking forward to engaging with many more of you in 2026.

## 2. CYP and VCSE Engagement

### Insights from Care-Experienced Young People

We held an engagement session on 18th November with care-experienced young people from CoramVoice to find out what they felt was important in establishing data standards in social care. Key themes that arose:

- **Privacy and choice**: Young people want control over sensitive information and the ability to opt out of sharing details they are uncomfortable with and with who that is.
- **Placements**: Young people want accurate, accessible details about placements, including local safety, and suitability for their needs.
- **Ongoing Engagement**: “Do with and not to” was a strong message— they want to stay involved with the project.

> “People need to access information that could protect you from yourself and from others.”

> “My care leavers team had no idea the area I moved into was dangerous.”

### Voluntary and Community Sector: A critical partner

Our roundtable with VCS leaders on 25th November reinforced their vital role in shaping and implementing data standards.  Among the many themes discussed:

- **AI & ethics**: Partners stressed that data standards (not guidance) are crucial for safe, meaningful AI adoption, helping useful tools spread faster while managing commercial, delivery and ethical risks.
- **Capacity**: Smaller organisations may struggle to keep pace with technical changes; clear support and integration pathways will be essential to avoid widening gaps.
- **Trust**: VCS groups are often the most trusted by families, making their involvement central to shaping national standards that work on the ground.

These insights will inform the next phase of standards development, ensuring that the voices of those most affected remain central to the project and achieving our aims.

Our spreadsheets have almost burst at the seams trying to keep track of the number of people, from different LAs, suppliers, and others who have joined our working groups during 2025. Thank you so much for your engagement!

### Behind the scenes of the interdependency web!

Our work on standards to enable MAIS or safeguarding has been a big focus but is, of course, tied up with related programmes on several things like, establishing a single unique identifier, designing infrastructure for identity resolution, message routing, and negotiating participation from partners outside of social care.

That’s inevitable in a programme this ambitious - by one (admittedly disputed) count we’re trying to index information across 30,000 systems and do it in a way which can secure acceptance from a similar number of data controllers. 

We can’t say much yet, but we’ve been working closely with the other DfE teams on this and are excited by what we’re seeing.

Hopefully all of you who’ve advised on issues such as implementation practicalities, IG, and what social workers really need will be able to see your imprint on the plans when they are shareable.  

### But our big achievement: The first standard is heading for mass adoption ahead of schedule!

V1 of the placement’s standard is ready to use! Even more exciting, 6/9 regions, representing over 100 LAs, have expressed an interest in implementing it next year. We were expecting a third of that, and it’s been great to see the enthusiasm there is amongst LAs for the ability to get a meaningful and reliable picture of regional placement sufficiency.

We’ve also had [CareCubed](https://carecubed.org/) pop up to tell us that they’re working the standard into their ebrokerage software and so we’ll need to bring forward work on software testing and accreditation. Apologies to Samu at ODI and the engineering team.

### And we progress forward with MAIS

On MAIS the last month has seen us slightly shift our attention away from standards development, and more towards reflecting on the standards we've developed in 2025 and thinking about where we want to focus our efforts in 2026.

The CYP engagement session, was an insightful conversation and challenged many of the design decisions we've taken as we've developed our standards.

### What we’re adding to on the Adult’s side

We are lucky to be chaired by Prof Sir Anthony Finklestein, and to have children’s expertise from Staffordshire DCS Bernie Brown as well as Cambridgeshire PSW Tapiwa Julius.

With the expansion of the programme to cover adult social care we have been joined by Mark Nicholas (Chief Social Worker at NHS Digital, and Clinical Director for the NHS App), among others. They’ll advise on where we should focus our efforts and hold us to account.


## 4. Looking Ahead to 2026

[Andrew](https://theodi.org/profile/andrew-newman/) at the Open Data Institute said when we started this ***‘We’ve got three years in which to do fifteen years’ work’***. That’s partly an encouragement to move fast, but it’s also a reminder that what we prioritise first is critical.

We started with Multi-Agency Safeguarding because of the moral urgency and the opportunity for larger impact presented by parallel work on a single unique identifier, and we added placements in response to steers from Directors of Children’s Services.

Going into 2026 we have a chance to broaden the slate of standards we are working on, not least to take on use cases which are priorities for adult social care. We look forward to sharing with you some of the use cases which have been identified in our research with LAs, and early thinking on how to prioritise amongst them.
