### Broader Programme Objectives

**Tom Rintoul (Social Finance)** provided background on the DfE-funded interoperability standards programme:
- **Goal:** Enable information created in one system to be accessed and used meaningfully in another, free from technical or commercial barriers.
- Linked to the creation of a single unique identifier, with the broader intent to realise the benefits of interoperability.
- **Four main benefit areas:**
  1. Safeguarding – relevant information in the right hands at the right time.
  2. Data analysis – particularly for sufficiency and strategic insight (**focus for this group**).
  3. Productivity – integrate third-party tools more easily.
  4. Power sharing – enable safe information-sharing with children and families.
- Placement data standard chosen early due to sector momentum, existing regional initiatives, and pressing sufficiency challenges.

### Data Standards Context

- Data standardisation is primarily an agreement between parties to describe things the same way, not just a technical exercise.
- Covers:
  - **Data standards** – consistent definitions and formats for information.
  - **Interoperability standards (APIs)** – ensuring data can move between systems.
- Programme duration: 3 years.
- Aim: Deliver **Version 1** of the placement standard in the next few months, with potential future iterations.
- Long-term goal: Establish institutions to maintain and evolve standards post-programme.

### Menti Poll Results

- **Representation:** Strong local authority (LA) and tech supplier presence; lower provider and data governance representation.
- **Roles:** Commissioners, data/analytics, directors, some transformation leads; few social workers or principal social workers.
- **Success factors:** Comparability, adoption, consistency, co-design, universality.
- **Obstacles:** Burden for under-resourced LAs, definitions/language, legacy systems, change management, data quality.


### Framing the Placement Sufficiency Challenge

**Madeline Goldie** summarised consultations with 46 LAs:
- Improving sufficiency analysis seen as a priority to improve child outcomes and reduce costs.
- **Scope:** Enable LAs to answer:
  - What type of provision is needed?
  - Where and in what volumes?
  - How to develop a business case with partners.
- **Out of scope (for now):** Contract monitoring, placement finding, care planning, outcomes tracking.

### Why the Placement Standard Matters

- Rising placement costs (~70% increase in 5 years; ~1,500 placements > £10k/week in 2023).
- ~50% of children in residential care placed >20 miles from home.
- Two “north star” problems:
  1. Unsustainable care market costs.
  2. Poor placement outcomes.

### Feedback and Questions (Part 1)

- **Supply vs. demand data:** It was noted that the current work focuses on demand-side data, while supply-side data issues remain significant.
- **Provider involvement:** The discussion highlighted the need for more provider engagement and for provider perspectives to be considered in the standard
- **Workflow & burden:** The importance of careful language was emphasised, recognising that not all areas have dedicated placement teams and that structures vary. The group cautioned against framing the work solely around speeding up processes, stressing that the priority should be ensuring tasks are done properly to the best standard. The standard should improve existing processes without adding unnecessary burdens and should fit within unique local workflows.
- **Mandation:**  A question was raised about whether adoption should be mandatory. It was noted that this is an ongoing conversation, but in most areas the Department is conscious that voluntary standards often fail due to the cost of change.

### Focused Use Case: Sufficiency Analysis

#### Draft Definition

> The ability to identify missing provision to minimise unnecessary sibling separation, out-of-area placements, and residential placements where foster was preferred, and build a business case for such provision.

#### Additional Analytical Questions Raised

- Matching preferred vs. actual placements.
- Whether local provision meets children’s needs.
- Cost vs. best option (value for money).
- Staffing models as a cost driver.
- Linking cost, need, and provision complexity.

[Video Snippet](https://vimeo.com/1108419810)


### Feedback and Questions (Part 2)

- **Analytical priorities:** The group discussed which analytical questions the standard should prioritise, including placement stability, cost vs. best option, staffing models, and supporting placement sufficiency strategies.
- **Value for money:** Some participants discussed the feasibility of analysing costs at the individual child level, noting the complexity of fee structures and the need for nuanced analysis.
- **Behaviour change:** A participant suggested considering desired behavioural shifts alongside data collection goals.
