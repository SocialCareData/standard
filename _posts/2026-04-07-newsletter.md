## Welcome

Since our last edition, we’ve seen strong progress across all three workstreams. As we move further into 2026, momentum is building, with growing adoption of our placements standard, deeper thinking on multi-agency information sharing (MAIS), and a clearer direction for Adult Social Care.

In this edition, we cover:
- Progress on placements
- Latest thinking on MAIS
- Moving forward on ASC
- What’s coming next, and how to get involved.

A huge thank you to everyone we have worked with, your insight and thought drives our work forward!

## 1. Placements – After a successful phase 1 pilot, we are rapidly approaching 50% of all LAs nationally using the standard by the Autumn

Having delivered a first release of the standard and a pilot in the South-West before Christmas, we are now rolling out the standard in Greater Manchester, the North East and South East.

There’s one last slot left for funded support from the team; if you are considering adopting the standard, now is the time to get in touch!


### Refining the standard based on real use

Based on what we’ve learnt in North East and Greater Manchester so far, we’ve made several refinements to standard including:

- **Location**: The free text field has been removed and replaced with an in/out LA field, an education continuity field, and a 'was it the preferred location?' field, giving clearer and more consistent location data.
- **Cost**: In response to an emerging consensus among Local Authorities, the total weekly fee has been split into four components, core, additional, education and other costs, to ensure placement costs are captured in a consistent way.
- **Quality assurance**: We are working with the North East on quality assurance and are awaiting the region's first data return, due on 13th April.

## 2. Standards Development

### 2.1 MAIS – Moving from standards that support systems to find out, “who else knows about a person,” to, “what do they know about that person?”

![pixelated to sharp image of the Mona Lisa](/assets/img/newsletter-4-graphic-mona-lisa.png)

Imagine each of the pixels above represents an information source accessed by a Social Worker. At present, Social Workers and safeguarding practitioners are generally at point 1: they have access to a handful of data sources and can’t be quite sure whether the picture of risk and service involvement that they’re seeing is an accurate one.

In an ideal world all social workers would be number 5, but with sharing each additional data source comes significant complexity, cost and challenges in information sharing.

So, our challenge as a programme over the last few months has been attempting to identify what a viable “tipping point” of information sharing would be for Social Workers – what is just the right amount of information that they would need to make informed decisions and have a clear picture of risk, without requiring so much information that sharing it would be unviable at national scale?

What we also need to create is a common language around describing the different information sources, for example: when a query is made to a system asking it to share back events information, both systems have a common understanding of what an “event” is in terms of data.

As a result, our list of standards has ballooned. You can see our full list, and offer comments and advice, over at [our GitHub](https://github.com/SocialCareData). For example, to see [a discussion on how we might model statuses](https://github.com/SocialCareData/person-standard/issues/59).

We are also hosting monthly Standards Development working groups to work through these issues, with the next one taking place on the 30th April at 14.30. Get in touch if you’d like an invitation to this session.


### 2.2 ASC – Setting our priorities to build standards that actually make a difference.

Our research and engagement have sharpened our focus: supporting truly joined-up care at neighborhood level, where better information flows directly and improves outcomes for people. Over the past few months, we’ve been working closely in the ASC sector to answer a simple, yet critical question:

> “Where can data standards make the biggest difference, soonest?”

Rather than starting from theory, we’ve grounded our priorities in the real challenges faced by local authorities, practitioners, and people drawing on care. What we have heard, consistently and clearly, is that fragmented information and disconnected systems are one of the biggest barriers to delivering joined-up, person-centred care.

#### A shared problem: fragmented systems, fragmented care

We successfully engaged and spoke to 8 DASS’ across local authorities within the East and West Midlands region, and Bath; 8 system leaders, and 17 individuals/groups across different roles - an impressive feat! - all of which told us the same problem: systems don’t talk to each other – even when the people using them do.

> “We’ve got practitioners working together, but the systems are completely separate – you end up with two versions of the truth.”

From hospital admission and discharges to information from mental health services, reablement providers, and care homes, critical updates are often delayed, incomplete or trapped in another system altogether. This is not a lack of ambition at local level, but a result of structural issues in case management systems, and the absence of common data standards.

#### Priority use cases: where standards can unlock joined-up care

From our engagement, a set of high-impact, shared use cases consistently rose to the top. These include:

- Accessing information to complete assessments and reviews without chasing other teams;
- Receiving timely notifications about hospital admissions and discharges;
- Supporting smooth transitions from CSC to ASC;
- Enabling structured recording and sharing of care needs assessments and care plans.

These are everyday moments in social care, but when systems don’t interoperate, they become a pain for practitioners, and risk for people.


#### Next steps

Our plans for this next phase are therefore to develop standards for the core elements of data that need to move around the system, e.g., standards for person, events, assessments, and plans. So that practitioners can make faster, better-informed decisions and deliver more coordinated care.

Across our DASS engagement, there has been strong backing for this direction, and a clear message that standards must lead to real implementation, not just good design. That insight feeds directly into our future implementation strategy. But it starts here – by prioritising standards that solve problems that LAs and practitioners are telling us about and doing so in partnership with the sector.


## 3. What’s coming next?

We’re heading into the next phase of the programme with a sharper focus on turning standards into everyday practice. Over the coming months, our work will centre on three things: deepening adoption, building the next set of priority standards, and making it easier for you to engage and influence what comes next.

We’ll continue our monthly working groups, publish drafts for comment on GitHub, and share clearer routes for contribution — whether that’s sense-checking emerging standards, joining pilots, or helping us pressure‑test implementation.

We’ll be sharing more detail on timings, drafts and opportunities to collaborate soon. If you want to be part of shaping the next phase of the programme, now is the perfect time to get involved.
