## Summary

Supporting access and contribution to a shared care record (from the data subject and family networks) in a way that accurately represents the person’s voice, experience and preferences.

    <ol style="font-weight: normal;" type="i">

    </ol>
  </li>

  <li>
    Individual (adult) contributing to their record
    <ol style="font-weight: normal;" type="i">

I need to update information about my preferences, routines, and communication needs, so that professionals understand how best to support me, and I need to approve, question, or correct information recorded about me, so that my record is accurate and reflects my perspective.</li>

## Description

Over recent years, there has been increased recognition across the social care sector of the importance of capturing the ‘voice of the child’ or ‘voice of an individual’ who is in receipt of care. For children and young people, participation at an age appropriate level empowers them and helps them to understand their context, even if they disagree with decisions being made to protect them.  This approach is both ethical and likely to lead to better outcomes for them.

For adults in receipt of care, control over what data is held and the ability to update and add to a record is even more important. In Adult Social Care, people often move between services (e.g., hospital, mental health, community support, home care). Without shared records, social workers and partners must rely on manual enquiries, repeated questions, or incomplete historical information. This creates risk, inefficiency, and frustration for the person. Data subjects should be able to add or verify information about what matters to them, their goals, routines, and preferences, which are core to Care Act–compliant assessments and person-centred support planning.

Challenge comes about in how this voice should be captured on a case management system which is predominantly designed to be a rigid record keeping system. There are a range of non-professional actors who need to contribute to a record (e.g. parents, carers, children), however at present there is not a consistent way across systems to give a mechanism to grant individuals access into the relevant parts of their record. Nor is there consistency in how this voice can be captured. As a result, the voice of the child/individual is usually limited to a free text box across different forms which the social worker will have to synthesise and write up, with more creative ways of expressing/capturing this voice going unexplored.

To summarise, the problem is twofold:

- CMS’ do not consistently support non-professional care actors from contributing towards a record
- The nature of their contribution is generally limited to free text, which is written by the social worker after speaking with the child/individual. This limits ‘true’ capture of a child/individual’s voice.  It is often recorded as part of a (for example) home visit record, making it difficult to locate and collate later.

CMS providers will argue that there is ‘portal’ functionality available at a cost to local authority, which allow for non-social workers to contribute to sections of a record. Anecdotal evidence suggests that this solution is ‘clunky’ and not particularly user friendly, but more research is needed here to understand the full scale and scope of portals as a solution. Regarding creative formats, CMS providers will argue that social workers/admin staff are able to upload documents/files (e.g. pictures of drawings) to the CMS, but these will typically be stored in the ‘documents’ section of a record and therefore not be immediately accessible when reviewing the sections of the record where the user voice has been captured.

Finally, more ‘family facing’ apps, such as Coram’s work on the Storyteller app, or 247 grid do not integrate well with CMS.

### Data

The notes could include:

- Record of experience

- Responses to specific assessments or plans

- Goals identified

- Preferences and communication needs

- Alternative media to capture voice of children/individuals, that integrates well with other forms/workflows within the CMS

### Benefits/Goal/Value/Purpose

When inputting in any form:

- Children and families’ input is taken into account when making decisions about them (including encouraging Family Group Decision Making processes)
- Individuals in receipt of care can see their records reflect their own voice and decisions

If able to directly input, then:

- Children, families and individuals in receipt of care have increased confidence that their voice is being heard and can see a direct link between this and the decisions that are made about them.
- Local authorities can monitor and evidence the extent to which children are families are being directly consulted and involved in the work undertaken with them.
- Social workers can use a variety of tools to capture the input from children/ individuals without having to duplicate effort (e.g. writing up and summarising an interview ).
- Children and families’ views are stored in consistent and searchable form

### Processes

Currently, this is typically done through the social worker, who will then document this on the care record. There are limited opportunities for subjects of care to directly input into their records.

This ‘double keying’ can be time intensive for the social worker, and dilutes the ‘user voice’ as in their write up the social worker might omit/alter certain points based on their unintended biases, or simply because as a professional they have different priorities and focus points.

## Landscape Review

### The challenge / opportunity

**Primary challenge**

- The voice of the child/family always needs to go through the social worker. Whilst technology exists that allows for children and families to input directly into their record (i.e. portals) it is not universally adopted and does not integrate well with case management systems.

**Secondary challenges**

- Family Group Decision Making (FGDM) is not supported where the social worker holds control and power over what is recorded on the CMS.
- Current systems for coproduction are hard to use and difficult to navigate.
- Digital divide and accessibility issues: Family members may have accessibility needs, learning difficulties, language barriers, psychological challenges, or other personality traits that create barriers to them accessing and contributing to secure records (e.g. via two-factor authentication) In addition, some may not have access to the right tools e.g. laptops, consistent internet access.

**Opportunity**

- Enable integration of ‘family facing’ digital tools that can capture the voice of the child and family, whether they use written, text, voice, photos, videos or drawing.
- Only appropriate information from the child’s record will be available during sessions with families.
- Outputs from these tools are stored within the CMS in the correct place and can be easily retrieved.
- Children, family and network members can input into co-produced records as and when they need to.

### Potential solutions

- Integration with third party providers/improved functionality of existing systems to support familial coproduction and input into records.
- Advanced user access and role profiling to change views and information displayed depending on who is accessing information.

Existing functionality and standards

- Note: Some CMS providers have ‘portals’ functionality that grants third party users access into the CMS.
- PRSB’s About Me and Core Information standards provide structured content for person-centred information.

## Out of Scope

- Any views on what might be out of scope here?
- Specific decisions about who is able to record what on a child’s case file are professional judgements that each CSC will want to make for themselves.  This might include individual social workers deciding whether to share a child protection plan with a child, based on the child’s age and capacity to understand.
- The balance between facilitating commenting on a plan vs coproducing a plan will also depend on the individual LA’s practice approach.
- Safety decisions – do we allow a parent to record a voice note that a young person who is in Care will then have access to listen to?
- Decisions about accessibility, although any user interface should have a high baseline of accessibility.
- Defining what should or should not be recorded / accessed by non-professionals; where / how that information should be stored; how content is moderated
- Describing the user interface

## Open Questions

- How effective ‘portals’ are in solving the alternative voice problem
- How to align with health e.g. NHS app
- What information should the data subject be able to edit, comment on, or view only?
