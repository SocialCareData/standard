## Summary

**Generic:** As a social care professional, I need to quickly find, understand, and reuse important information stored across the CMS, so that I can make informed decisions, complete required documentation efficiently, and provide high‑quality support without unnecessary administrative burden.

**Examples**

As a social worker, I need to find information within my CMS quickly and reliably, so that I can use it to inform mine or my colleagues planning and work with the subject of care.

As a social worker, I need to synthesise existing information which is spread throughout many fields and documents, into new content for new and specific purposes.

As a social worker, I need to be able to find patterns in existing records that go back multiple years and are recorded using different form templates and linguistic and analytical styles.  I need this information to be rank-able chronologically and by level of importance.

As a social worker I need to be able to see the most important information quickly and easily.

As a social worker I need the form that I am currently completing to automatically contain information that is already captured in other places in the CMS, both to aid my analysis and to reduce duplication of effort.

As a manager / auditor, I need to find key information (including specific documents) quickly and easily to conduct case file audits.

## Description

Case Management Systems in social care are best understood as a digitisation of a paper-based records management system. For each case, a set of forms will need to be completed, with a range of other documents and case notes coming together to create the child/adult’s record. Some forms need to be completed before others are started (e.g. each case must start with a contact, which then will become a referral, which will then become an assessment – this is termed as a ‘workflow’).

As a result, for each case a lot of information is captured across a range of forms, documents and case notes. When a range of scenarios demand that social workers gather that information, this exercise can take a lot of time, reducing the time that social workers get to spend with families, and key information can be missed, increasing risks to the case subjects. Discovering the information is one half of the challenge; once information is found it has to be effectively synthesised, with clear chronologies of what happened when documented and presented. This can be difficult when information is hidden away across systems and chronologies are not auto-populated.

Our research with social workers identified that search functionality within case management systems is limited, and the navigability/usability of these systems is frequently cited by social workers as a key challenge in their use of these systems. For example, Mosaic does not have a ‘back’ button, making it difficult for social workers to navigate between pages when quickly trying to gather information.

All of this leads to frustration for social workers, who believe that information held within their case management system should be more discoverable, so they can spend more time on the more important parts of their job.

### Data

Finding data, e.g. records of events, secondary care encounters, placements, related persons, key events, quotes

Synthesising e.g. notes about why someone is in care, recent events, for one of the purposes below

### Benefits

Finding information enables many purposes e.g.:

- Producing outputs for children
- Analysing patterns of behaviour and risk over time
- Arriving at decisions
- Sharing information with other agencies

Synthesis of information is required for many purposes e.g.:

- Production of chronologies
- Case summaries for internal reporting
- Reports to courts and other agencies
- Requests to other agencies for information, coordination, or resources
- Briefing materials for someone about to start work with the person (e.g. new SW, care provider, or other service)

### Processes

Processes for finding and retrieving information can be resource intensive at present. Social workers have to spend a lot of time clicking through individual forms, documents and case notes to find information that they then have to extract.

## Landscape Review

North Yorkshire Council are a key example here of where the council has used AI effectively to support this challenge. There, they took a copy of the case management system then utilised Microsoft AI tools to support with discovery and synthesis of information across a range of file formats.

### The challenge / opportunity

**The primary problem**

- Social workers spend too much time at their desk on tasks that do not support their practice or the children and families they are supporting.

**The secondary problem**

- Case management systems replicate paper-based systems, consequently making it hard to find specific information across large case files. Information is captured haphazardly cross multiple places
- Social workers need to synthesise a lot of information to produce key outputs (e.g. chronologies), arrive at decisions concerning the child/their family, and when deciding to share information with other agencies.
- Social workers spent quite significant amounts of time copy-pasting information from old forms into new ones because of absent or ineffective prepopulation of fields.

For Social Worker information retrieval, we envision a world where:

- Social workers can easily find the information that they need when they need to produce outputs for children, arrive at decisions and share information with other agencies.
- Tasks which require the synthesis of large amounts of information (e.g. the production of chronologies) become more straightforward and less burdensome.

### Potential solutions

- Integration with third party providers/improved functionality of existing systems to support improved search, summarisation and prioritisation of information, potentially using AI tools to support.
- Integration with third party providers/improved functionality of existing systems for specific outputs (e.g. genograms and chronologies).

This could lead to:

- The user experience for social workers when using their case management systems improves, increasing their capacity to assess risk.
- Children and families spend more time with their social workers and have a reduced need to repeat information.
- A thriving market for tools which integrate with CMS’ emerges, compounding these benefits.
- Data collated from different agencies allows for a timelier and more accurate assessment of risk.
