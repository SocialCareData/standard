**National Placement Standards Program Overview:**
Ivjyot provided an overview of the National Placement Standards programme, highlighting its aim to standardise placement data across local authorities, reduce duplication, and improve sufficiency analysis while minimising administrative burden.
- The programme forms part of a three-year DfE-funded initiative delivered with Coram and the Open Data Institute.
- It aims to improve the consistency of placement data while making use of existing statutory returns and avoiding changes to local case management systems.
- Participants were encouraged to engage with the programme through the Social Care Standards website and GitHub.

**Regional Implementation Updates:**
Disha coordinated updates from regional leads including Kim (Northeast), Ellen (Greater Manchester), and Daryl and Joseph (Southeast), who described their progress, challenges, and learnings in implementing the Placement Standards across their respective regions.
- **Northeast Region Update:** Kim and Caroline reported that the Northeast region, with Durham as the lead authority, has completed its first quarter of data collection using the Placement Standards, developed a regional Power BI dashboard, and iterated on the data template to improve data quality and usability. They highlighted collaborative work with Social Finance and the importance of strategic leadership support. Kim detailed the technical process: each local authority submits data via a secure Teams channel for data submission, with a designated data lead in each authority responsible for collating and validating data before automated extraction and processing. Automated DQ (data quality) checks and feedback loops help improve data quality, and the Power BI dashboard provides both aggregate and row-level views for users.
- **Greater Manchester Combined Authority Update:** Ellen shared that Greater Manchester is engaging its ten local authorities through one-to-one meetings to onboard them, address capacity challenges, and streamline data collection. The region is working to integrate the Placement Standards with existing data sets and platforms, aiming for a common data model.
- **Southeast Region Update:** Daryl and Joseph described the Southeast's early-stage implementation, including securing DCS approval, engaging commissioners, and updating data protection agreements. They are considering additional data fields and aligning their collection process with the Placement Standards, with plans to use a secure platform for data uploads.

**Technical and Data Collection Challenges:**
The group discussed technical challenges such as handling unregistered placements, categorizing residential family assessment centers, and ensuring data quality and consistency across local authorities.
- Kim explained that unregistered placements are captured in the provider URN field, with clarifications added in version 2 of the data template to distinguish between missing data and unregistered status. The group discussed the sensitivity of surfacing this data and the need for clear guidance.
- Ida raised concerns about how residential family assessment centers are categorized, noting their differences from standard residential placements. Kim acknowledged the limitations of the current standard and indicated that such nuances may be addressed in future iterations.

**Learnings and Best Practices from Implementation:**
The group shared key learnings from regional pilots, including the importance of local leadership, the need for coordination across teams, and the value of iterative template development and strategic advocacy.
- Ivjyot emphasized that successful implementation often depends on appointing an accountable lead within each local authority to coordinate fragmented data across teams, especially in larger authorities with high placement volumes.
- Kim and others highlighted the importance of iterating the data collection template based on user feedback, particularly to clarify ambiguous fields and improve data quality. The process benefits from close collaboration with social finance and local authorities.
- Caroline and Kim noted that strategic leadership from DCSs and commissioning leads is critical for driving participation and overcoming challenges, such as the time required for data entry and the need for resource alignment.

**Design and Governance of the Placement Standard:**
The group discussed the governance framework for evolving the Placement Standard, including the introduction of core, bespoke, and proposed changes buckets, and the criteria for adding or modifying data fields.
- Ivjyot introduced a governance model with three buckets: core standard (nationally required fields), bespoke (region-specific fields), and proposed changes (fields under consideration for national adoption). This structure aims to balance standardization with regional flexibility.
- The proposed criteria for adding these new fields to the core standard is that they should be relevant to the mission, feasible to implement, impose low administrative burden, and avoid unintended consequences.
- Madeline and Ivjyot encouraged participants to use GitHub to propose changes and provide feedback, with the working group serving as the main forum for discussion and consensus-building. Proposed changes will be reviewed and, if agreed, incorporated into future versions of the standard.
- Ida and others cautioned against excessive bespoke customization, noting the maintenance challenges and the risk of missing opportunities for national standardization. The group agreed to test regional needs before designating fields as bespoke.

**Limitations, Data Use, and DfE Engagement:**
The group discussed the limitations of the Placement Standard, the importance of communicating these to DfE, and the need to manage expectations about what the data can and cannot achieve.
- Caroline raised concerns about the inability of the current data set to answer certain strategic questions, such as the number of out-of-region children in placements, due to limitations in data from external providers. Madeline confirmed that the group serves as the mechanism to communicate such gaps to DfE.
- Michael warned that DfE or others might misinterpret or overextend the use of the data, particularly around costs and benchmarking, without understanding its limitations. Madeline agreed and emphasized the need for clear documentation and caveats.
- Ida referenced a previous failed attempt to standardize needs and provision data, highlighting the challenges of engaging providers and the complexity of cost breakdowns. Marie added that contract clauses can significantly affect placement costs, complicating benchmarking efforts.

**Next Steps:**
Madeline outlined the next steps for the program, including the upcoming release of the next standard version, plans for a community of practice, and exploration of additional data standards such as fostering pipeline data and multi-agency information sharing.
- Ivjyot and Madeline announced that the next version of the Placement Standard will be released September, with a draft circulated for feedback at least four weeks prior. Participants were encouraged to review the specification on GitHub and provide input.
- Madeline proposed establishing a community of practice to connect stakeholders working on data standards across the children's social care sector, aiming to share knowledge, map ongoing initiatives, and potentially inform the standards program pipeline.
- The team is considering prioritizing new data standards, particularly around the fostering pipeline, to match demand and supply data. Input from the group will be sought, and working groups may be formed for specific topics.
- Madeline noted that another strand of the program is focused on multi-agency information sharing and the development of a single unique identifier, with opportunities for interested participants to join related working groups.
