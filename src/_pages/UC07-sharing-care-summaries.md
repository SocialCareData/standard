## Summary

As a social work admin support team, I need to be able to create a version of someone’s care records which are intelligible to them and do not breach duties of confidentiality to others, so that I can uphold the LA’s ethical and legal responsibility to them.  I need to compile and share them in a way that minimises the traumatising impact of reading them on the person.As an adult who has left the care system, I need to access a copy of my records which I can understand, so I can understand what decisions were made about me and why.

## Description

Young people have a legal right to access their CSC case records from the age of 18.  Those that do generally have a poor experience of accessing and reading them.  Some of the reasons for this are:

1. Records are very long and compiled in confusing ways, with only small amounts of genuinely meaningful information (e.g. a 30 page assessment may only have a few sentences of new information).

2. There are challenges around data protection that mean records need to be redacted if they mention individuals who aren’t the person in question).  Local Authorities don’t have the capacity to rearrange or anonymise these, so they redact large portions instead (e.g. one adult accessed her records and received 900 pages, 95% of which was black ink). LAs tend to be quite heavy handed about this, since historically they have not secured explicit consent from individuals for their data to be shared. However, if a person was known to the child, their name does not need to be redacted, but it is often less effort to do this. What does need redacting is any personal information that the child should not know about, but this can also cause issues with areas like family medical history. Similarly, GDPR can have negative consequences

3. Local authority capacity and capability for synthesising and summarising records that have grown over years of service involvement.  Records will be written in various styles, using a variety of jargon as practice has changed over time, and by multiple social workers with different recording styles.   The process of creating a redacted record takes a lot of admin time, through finding material to include, working out what to record, identifying what should be redacted.

4. Records are often written to justify actions based on level of risk to the child, for an intended audience of other professionals.  They focus in detail on harm, risks, and family failings, and can be pathologizing.  Stylistically they can be judgemental, and they often fail to convey empathy, particularly to parents.  Many adults who read their case files find it pathologizing, judgemental and re-traumatising (see link to MIRRA Project research).

Young people however have a right to their records after they leave the care system, to understand what decisions were made about them and why. This arduous, admin heavy activity theoretically could be supported through the use and application of AI tools, designed to synthesise and draw out key themes from large volumes of information. However, the sensitive nature of the information being reviewed by these tools, and the importance of getting these records ‘right’ for care leavers poses a significant risk for the application of untested tools / a strong argument for a professional ‘human in the loop’.

### Data

Records will include information from case files containing care leaver’s personal data.

This could include:

- Case notes, visits and conversation records (including email exchanges)
- Assessments and plans
- Information about placements, school, medical information
- Key decisions and rationale
- Care leaving information
- Photos, etc.

Some data will need to be excluded from the record shared with the care leaver, particularly as requests are made under the DPA. For example, personal data relating to third parties may be excluded.

### Benefits

Access to records with comprehensive information and a clear story can help care leavers in a number of ways including:

- Understanding their personal history and family background
- Understanding decisions made about them, including reasons why they were in care.  Psychologically, this sense-making process is an important part of releasing emotional trauma.
- To try and contact those who were significant during their time in care or relatives
- Medical information

### Processes

Care leavers typically request their records through a Subject Access Request under the Data Protection Act (2018). Processes for implementing these requests vary across LAs. The process of creating a version of records to share with care leavers is admin intensive, as it requires social workers to review what information needs to be redacted and consider how to create something that it intelligible to the recipient.

## Landscape Review

### The challenge / opportunity

**The primary problem**

- The process for care leavers of accessing their children’s social care record is a difficult experience and can be re-traumatising.

**The secondary problem**

- There is often a significant time lag between requesting records and receiving them. When they arrive, they are significantly redacted and can be of poor quality.
- The process of pulling together a subject access request (SAR) for care leavers is time consuming, requires multiple contacts and puts a lot of pressure on the social work teams.

For care leavers attempting to access their records, we envision a world where:

- Data about children is standardisable, searchable and retrievable, easing the burden on social workers responding to care leavers requesting access to their records.
- Those receiving access to the records receive quality outputs with clear information in a timely way.
- Less time spent in individual data processing allows resources to be transferred to more professional support for those receiving access to their records.

### Potential solutions

- Integration with third party providers/improved functionality of existing systems to support search, summarisation and prioritisation including AI tools.
- Integration with third party providers/improved functionality of existing systems to support with specific tasks (e.g. redaction) including AI tools.

This could lead to:

- Improved experience of people accessing their records once they leave care.
- Time savings for social workers and support staff who need to redact records.
